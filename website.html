The Geography of Educational Privilege

A data-driven exploration of how independent schools shape — and reflect — inequality across England

1,613 Independent Schools

Across England, over 1,600 independent schools educate approximately 7% of all pupils.

But their distribution tells a story of geographic inequality.

Each point represents one school. Color indicates gender composition: Mixed (green), Boys (blue), Girls (pink).

London's Concentration

Zooming into London reveals a remarkable density of independent schools.

The capital contains a disproportionate share of private education, particularly in affluent boroughs like Westminster, Kensington & Chelsea, and Richmond.

This clustering reflects — and reinforces — broader patterns of wealth and opportunity.

The Clarendon Nine

At the pinnacle of this system sit the Clarendon Schools — nine historic institutions defined by the 1861 Clarendon Commission.

20 Prime Ministers

educated at Eton College alone.

These schools have shaped British politics, law, and culture for centuries, representing the most elite tier of an already exclusive system.

Access & Opportunity

As someone who attended private school on a full bursary, I've seen firsthand how transformative access to quality education can be.

But the geographic concentration of these institutions raises important questions about equality of opportunity in the UK education system.
