Does Private Education Create Advantage or Reflect It?

A data-driven investigation into education and inequality in the UK

Aims

This project investigates whether independent schools in England create educational advantages or simply reflect existing socioeconomic privilege. Whilst independent schools consistently demonstrate superior academic outcomes, the central question examines whether this performance gap represents genuine value-added education or geographic clustering in already-advantaged areas. Through geographic analysis, historical cost trends, academic performance comparisons, and statistical correlation, this research explores the complex relationship between private education, inequality, and opportunity.

Geographic Clustering and Inequality

Geographic Distribution

England's 1,613 independent schools are unevenly distributed across the country, with significant clustering in the South East and London.

London's Concentration

Zooming into London reveals how independent schools cluster in wealthier boroughs with lower deprivation indices, particularly in West and Central London.

This geographic pattern raises a critical question: are independent schools clustered in high-performing areas because they improve them, or because privilege was already concentrated there?

Disentangling Correlation from Causation

Testing whether private schools create or reflect advantage

Establishing the Correlation

Initial analysis revealed a moderate positive correlation (r=0.380, p=0.032) between independent school density and state school GCSE performance across London boroughs. However, this correlation could reflect two competing explanations:

Hypothesis A (Creation): Independent schools generate local benefits through competition, spillover effects, or raised standards.

Hypothesis B (Reflection): Independent schools locate in already-privileged areas where affluent, educated families—and consequently strong state schools—are already concentrated.

Testing Through Multiple Regression

To distinguish between these explanations, I extended the analysis by controlling for four socioeconomic factors: median household income, adults with degree qualifications, average house prices, and the Index of Multiple Deprivation. The question became: after accounting for existing privilege structures, does independent school density still predict state school performance?

The Results

The multiple regression revealed a dramatic shift:

  • Simple model: Coefficient = 0.380 (R² = 0.145)
  • Controlled model: Coefficient = 0.111 (R² = 0.504)
  • Change: 71% reduction in effect size

The model's explanatory power more than tripled (R² = 0.504), with socioeconomic factors—particularly deprivation (coefficient: -0.866) and parental education (coefficient: 0.103)—emerging as far stronger predictors than independent school density.

Interpretation

These results support Hypothesis B: independent schools predominantly reflect rather than create geographic advantage. Once we account for family wealth, parental education, and area deprivation, independent school density contributes minimally to state school outcomes. The apparent correlation in the simple model was spurious—both variables are products of underlying privilege structures rather than one causing the other.

Independent schools cluster where families already possess the economic, cultural, and social capital that predicts academic success. At the borough level, private education reflects existing advantage rather than creating it.

Data

Data was sourced from multiple UK government datasets and historical archives. Independent school locations were obtained from the Department for Education's Get Information About Schools (GIAS) service, providing comprehensive establishment data including coordinates, pupil numbers, and institutional characteristics. Academic performance data (GCSE and A-level results by institution type) came from the DfE's Explore Education Statistics platform, covering years 2019–2025. University progression statistics were accessed through the DfE's Higher Education progression database. Geographic boundary data for mapping utilised ONS Geography Portal shapefiles and London Datastore resources. Historical fee data for the Clarendon Schools was manually compiled from archival sources and adjusted for inflation using Bank of England historical inflation calculators. Socioeconomic data for the multiple regression analysis was obtained from the Office for National Statistics, including median household income, educational qualifications, house prices, and Index of Multiple Deprivation scores at borough level.

All data processing was conducted in Python using Google Colab notebooks (accessible at this link), with cleaned datasets hosted publicly on GitHub for reproducibility. Whilst initial attempts to automate data collection through the ONS API were explored, direct CSV downloads proved more reliable for this project's scope, ensuring data quality and consistency across multiple years.

Tools & Methodology

Data cleaning and analysis were performed using Python (pandas, scipy.stats, sklearn) within Google Colab environments. Geographic coordinates required transformation from British National Grid to WGS84 latitude/longitude for web mapping compatibility. Historical fee data underwent inflation adjustment using Bank of England multipliers to convert all values to 2025 prices, enabling meaningful temporal comparisons. Statistical correlation analysis employed Pearson's correlation coefficient and ordinary least squares regression through scipy.stats.linregress to quantify the relationship between independent school density and local state school performance. Multiple linear regression was conducted using sklearn.linear_model.LinearRegression to control for confounding socioeconomic variables.

Visualisations were built using Vega-Lite specifications, chosen for their declarative approach and interactive capabilities. Each chart incorporates interactive elements—dropdown menus for metric selection, temporal sliders, and hover tooltips—to enhance data exploration. The project website is hosted via GitHub Pages, with all visualisation specifications, cleaned datasets, and analytical code publicly accessible in the repository, ensuring full reproducibility and transparency.

Key technical challenges included handling missing historical data (some schools lacked complete fee records across all years), managing data type mismatches during borough-level merges (GSS codes vs. numeric LA codes), and optimising map rendering performance with 1,600+ data points. These were addressed through strategic data filtering, explicit type conversions, and layered visualisation approaches that balance detail with performance.

Conclusions

This analysis reveals that whilst independent schools demonstrably produce superior academic outcomes—with performance gaps of 20–30 percentage points at GCSE and A-level, and nearly triple the university access rates to elite institutions—these advantages cannot be attributed to school quality alone. The multiple regression analysis provided compelling evidence that the observed correlation between independent school concentration and state school performance (r=0.380, R²=0.145, p=0.032) is largely spurious. When controlling for socioeconomic factors, the independent school coefficient dropped by 71%, whilst the model's explanatory power more than tripled (R²=0.504). This indicates that private schools cluster in already-advantaged geographic areas where state provision is comparatively strong.

The answer to whether private education creates advantage or reflects it appears to be: both. Independent schools do deliver measurably better examination results and university access, justifying their positioning as high-performing institutions. However, these schools concentrate in wealthy areas with strong local state sectors, suggesting that the "independent school premium" partly represents the purchase of access to advantaged geographic and social networks rather than purely superior pedagogy. The statistical evidence demonstrates that area-level deprivation and parental education are far stronger predictors of educational outcomes than independent school presence.

Moreover, the historical cost analysis demonstrates that elite education is becoming progressively less accessible. Even after inflation adjustment, Clarendon School fees have increased substantially in real terms over 95 years, concentrating access amongst increasingly narrow socioeconomic bands. Combined with the geographic clustering findings, this suggests that private education simultaneously reflects existing privilege whilst potentially amplifying inequality through restricted access and concentrated opportunity networks.

Future research could extend this analysis by examining long-term earnings outcomes, controlling for prior attainment in university access analysis, or investigating whether the geographic correlation holds at finer spatial scales. Additionally, comparative analysis across different types of independent schools (highly selective vs. less selective, day vs. boarding) might reveal whether value-added effects vary by institutional characteristics.
